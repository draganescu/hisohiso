Most apps are secure. Almost none are quiet.

Most messengers are secure, but not deeply private. Accounts, phone numbers, nicknames, contact discovery, invites, forwarding, groups, and cross-device history are useful. They also create metadata and social surfaces that make you easier to find, follow, and correlate.

Human chat

Simple and raw on purpose

Open a channel, send the link, approve knocks from the people who should be there. No phone number, profile, contact graph, searchable identity, or server-side readable history.

The tradeoff

Less convenient, more private

Hisohiso is not trying to be a better social messenger. It is inconvenient in the places where convenience usually costs privacy.

A private agent deserves a private line.

Agent chat in Slack, WhatsApp, Telegram, or a vendor app is still ordinary chat: lots of typing, weak task UI, and a third party in the middle. Hisohiso is purpose-built for local agents: one channel per session, touch-friendly replies, and the same end-to-end privacy model used for humans.

Not a cockpit. A channel.

Products such as Paseo and Happy are closer to full mobile coding-agent cockpits. They are useful when you want provider orchestration, worktrees, schedules, voice, GitHub flows, or native app polish. Hisohiso chooses the smaller surface: one accountless encrypted room that can carry human chat, agent approvals, diffs, command output, and short decisions.

Choose a cockpit when

You want agent management

Use a full agent product when the main job is managing many coding agents, branches, providers, PRs, schedules, or desktop/mobile clients.

Choose hisohiso when

You want the line private

Use hisohiso when the important primitive is an encrypted, disposable, self-hostable channel between your phone, people, and local tools.

What it refuses to do is the point.

  • No signup. You do not need to hand over an email address or phone number before you can talk.
  • No server-side readable history. Messages live in your browser storage. The server routes ciphertext.
  • No social layer. No profiles, contact graph, read receipts, feeds, forwarding network, or little engagement tricks.
  • Self-hostable router. The repo ships with Docker Compose, FrankenPHP/Caddy, Mercure, and SQLite; even the default router only sees encrypted traffic.

For people who read the code before they trust it.

Hisohiso is small on purpose. The promise is simple: the server should not read the channel, the channel should not require an identity, and the same private line should work whether you are talking to a person, a local coding agent, Hermes, or a local model on a private machine.

If that tradeoff sounds like you, open a channel. If you'd rather see the wiring first, start with the security page.
